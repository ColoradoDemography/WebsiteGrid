Colorado Results from the American Community Survey 2019-2023

Introduction

On December 12, 2025, the Census Bureau released the 2019-2023 American Community Survey (ACS). This dataset contains population characteristics for Census block groups, census tracts, municipalities, counties, and states. This data provides a rich picture of Colorado educational attainment, income, housing, and work location, among other social characteristics.

It is important to note the ACS 5-year estimates are not designed to measure rapid change during short periods because the data come from a 5-year period. The most appropriate comparison for the 2019-2023 ACS release is the 2014-2018 ACS or prior years as they do not include overlapping years. Dollar values are in real (inflation-adjusted) values for the last year of the 5-year series (for example, all data in the 2019-2023 series are in 2023 dollars).

The ACS 5-year data SHOULD NOT be used for total population estimates since it is based on 5 years of data. Please visit the State Demography Office Homepage for population estimates and forecasts.

Income and Poverty

State Results

  • Median household income increased between the 2014-2018 ACS and the 2019-2023 ACS. In inflation-adjusted dollars, median household income increased from $83,763 to $92,740.
  • The percentage of people in poverty fell between the 2014-2018 ACS and 2019-2023 ACS. The number of people with incomes below 100 percent of the federal poverty level in the 2014-2018 ACS was 591,000, or 10.9 percent, while it was 534,000, or 9.4 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS.
  • The number of people with incomes below 200 percent of the federal poverty level in the 2014-2018 ACS was 1.4 million or 26.5 percent, while it was 1.3 million, or 22.5 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS.

The ACS 5-year data does not provide much detail about the pattern of change in the poverty rate. Looking at the ACS 1-year data for Colorado between 2014 and 2023, the percentage of people with incomes below 100 percent of the federal poverty level varied between 12.0 percent in 2014 to 9.3 percent in 2023. With the exception of 2021, which reflected the poverty level during COVID-19 pandemic and recession, the percentage of people with incomes below the poverty level shows a gradual decline.

County Results

  • Median household income increased significantly in 29 counties between 2014-2018 and 2019-2023. The median increase across counties was $11,000 in 2023 Dollars. The largest increases were seen in Lake, Dolores, Custer, Rio Grande, and Archuleta counties.
  • The percentage of people living below 100 percent of the federal poverty level decreased in 16 counties and increased in four counties. The largest percentage reduction was seen in Crowley, Lake, Conejos, and Hinsdale counties. Four counties saw increases in the poverty level: La Plata, Gilpin, Bent, and Phillips.

Housing Ownership and Renting and Housing Costs

State Results

  • The homeownership rate for Colorado rose by 1.4 percentage points from 64.9 percent in the 2014-2018 ACS to 66.3 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS and the number of owner-occupied households increased by 171,000.
  • Housing value and housing costs increased, in inflation-adjusted dollars, between the 2014-2018 and 2019-2023 ACS.
  • The median home value of owner-occupied housing units increased from $382,600 to $502,900.
  • The number of owner-occupied housing units with a mortgage decreased from 72 percent to 70 percent.
  • The median monthly housing costs for owner-occupied housing units with a mortgage decreased by approximately $400, from $2,135 to $1,754.
  • The median monthly gross rent increased from $1,456 to $1,693.
  • The percentage of homeowners paying more than 30 percent of their income on housing costs increased from 22.8 percent in 2014-2018 to 24.0 percent in 2019-2023. The percentage of renters paying more than 30 percent of their income on housing costs increased slightly from 48.5 percent in the 2014-2018 ACS to 49.8 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS.

County Results

  • Median home values increased between the 2014-2018 and 2019-2023 ACS in 53 counties. The median increase, in 2023 dollars, was approximately $92,000. The smallest increase, $19,000, was in Conejos County, while the largest increase, $372,000, was in Pitkin County.
  • The median monthly housing costs for owner-occupied housing units with a mortgage decreased between the 2014-2018 and 2019-2023 ACS in 43 counties. The median decrease, in 2023 dollars, was $290. The smallest decrease, $100, was in Dolores County, while the largest decrease, $550, was in Douglas County.
  • The median gross rent increased between the 2014-2018 and 2019-2023 ACS in 24 counties, decreased in one county (San Miguel County), and remained statistically unchanged in the rest of the counties. The median increase, in 2023 dollars, was slightly over $200. The smallest monthly increase, $80, was in Douglas County, while the largest increase, just under $600, was in Park County.

Percentage of People Working from Home

State Results

The 2019-2023 ACS includes data for the 2020-2021 COVID-19 pandemic. The percentage of persons who report working from home more than doubled, compared to the pre-pandemic 2014-2018 ACS. In the 2014-2018 ACS 7.7 percent of people age 16 and older reported working from home. In the 2019-2023 ACS 18.8 percent of people age 16 and older reported working from home, a percentage increase of 144 percent.

County Results

The percentage of people working from increased in 32 counties, decreased in one county (Yuma County), and remained statistically unchanged in the rest of the counties. The median percentage increase between the 2014-2018 ACS and the 2019-2023 ACS was 8.9 percent. The largest increase was in San Juan County, where the percentage of people reporting working from home increased from 4.9 percent in the 2014-2018 ACS to 28.0 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS. The smallest increase was in Garfield County, where the percentage of people reporting working from home increased from 7.4 percent in the 2014-2018 ACS to 10.0 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS.

In the Front Range counties (Adams, Arapahoe, Boulder, Broomfield, Denver, Douglas, El Paso, Jefferson, Larimer, Pueblo, Teller and Weld counties), the median percentage of persons reporting working from home in the 2019-2023 ACS was 19 percent. The largest percentage was in Boulder and Douglas counties (28 percent and 27 percent, respectively) and the smallest percentages was in Pueblo County (8 percent). Twenty-four percent of persons in the City and County of Denver reported working from home in the 2019-2023 ACS.

Percentage of People Working Outside their County of Residence

A new set of tables for the 2019-2023 ACS reports the percentage of working age residents working in Colorado, but in a different county from their county of residence. This tabulation provides a general insight into commuting patterns. These tables do not caprute commuting flows (i.e., they do not show where people working outside of thier county are employed). Communting flows data are available from the Census Bureau's 'On the Map' application.

State Results

Overall, 99 percent of Colorado ACS respondents report living and working in Colorado, and 28.5 percent of respondents report working outside of their county of residence.

County Results

Among individual counties, the median percentage of respondents reporting working outside their county of residence was 15 percent. In five countries (Adams, Broomfield, Crowley, Elbert, and Park counties) more than 50 percent of respondents report working outside of their county of residence. These counties are mix of suburban counties in the Front Range (Adams and Broomfield counties) and more rural counties (Crowley, Elbert, and Park counties). Elbert County has the highest percentage of respondents working their county of residence (62.4 percent) and San Miguel County has the lowest percentage of respondents working their county of residence (1.8 percent).

Educational Attainment

State Results

  • For Colorado, the percentage of people age 25 and older reporting graduating from high school or earning a higher degree increased from 91.4 percent in 2014-2018 to 92.8 percent in 2019-2023.
  • The percentage of people age 25 and older with a bachelor’s degree or higher degree increased from 40.1 percent in the 2014-2018 ACS to 44.7 percent in the 2019-2023 ACS. In both the 2014-2018 ACS and the 2019-2023 ACS, the percentage of persons with at least a bachelor’s degree was second highest in the nation.
  • The percentage of current residents born in Colorado with a bachelor’s degree was 34.9 percent in both the 2014-2018 ACS and the 2019-2023 ACS. Colorado ranks fifth in the country for the share of the population born in state of residence with a bachelor’s degree or higher.
  • There is a significant difference in the share of the population with a bachelor’s degree or higher based on place of birth. This is consistent across the US. For the US, those living in the state of birth, 30.9 percent have a bachelor’s degree or higher, compared to those living outside the state of birth, 43.6 percent have a bachelor’s degree or higher.

County Results

  • The percentage of people age 25 and older with a bachelor’s degree or higher degree increased in 33 counties between the 2014-2018 ACS and the 2019-2023 ACS. The largest percentage increases were seen in Lincoln, Jackson, and Washington counties, each of which saw the percentage of people with a bachelor’s degree or higher increase by more than 50 percent.
    • Across all counties the median percentage of the population with a bachelor’s degree or higher was 31 percent. Boulder, Pitkin, San Miguel, and Douglas counties all have more than 60 percent of their population with a bachelor’s degree or higher, the highest among counties.
